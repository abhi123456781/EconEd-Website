How to Start a Chapter

1. Build Your Team

Gather a core team of 3-5 committed volunteers who share your passion for economic education. Look for people with diverse skills, including economic knowledge, teaching experience, and organizational abilities.

2. Identify Venues

Find suitable locations for your workshops and events. Schools, libraries, community centers, and religious institutions often have spaces available for educational programs. Look for venues that are accessible and can accommodate your planned activities.

3. Build Local Partnerships

Connect with organizations in your community, such as schools, youth centers, and local businesses. These partnerships can help you reach students who would benefit from your programs and potentially provide resources or support.

4. Contact EconEd Headquarters

Reach out to us to officially register your chapter. We'll provide you with our Chapter Starter Kit, which includes curriculum materials, training resources, branding guidelines, and administrative support to help you get started.

5. Launch Your Chapter

Plan and host your first event or workshop. Start small and focus on quality over quantity. Gather feedback from participants and volunteers to continuously improve your programs.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much time does it take to run a chapter?

The time commitment varies depending on the size and frequency of your programs. Most chapter leaders spend 5-10 hours per week on chapter activities, including planning, coordination, and teaching.

Is there a cost to start a chapter?

There is no fee to start an EconEd chapter. We provide the curriculum and materials at no cost. Chapters are responsible for securing their own venues and equipment, though many find partners who donate these resources.

How many volunteers do I need?

We recommend starting with at least 3-5 core volunteers. As your chapter grows, you may want to recruit more volunteers to accommodate more participants and offer more programs.

Do I need economics expertise to start a chapter?

While some economic knowledge is helpful, it's not required for all chapter leaders. What's most important is organizational ability, passion for the mission, and the ability to recruit volunteers with the necessary skills.
